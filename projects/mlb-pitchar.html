Project 04 · Mobile AR Learning

MLB PitchAR

"How to turn confusion into curiosity." A mobile AR application that helps rookie baseball fans build intuitive pitch recognition through spatial experience.

Design

01

Context & Challenge

Baseball faces declining youth engagement — only 7% of Americans under 30 name it as their favorite sport. Game structure changes (longer games, more strikeouts, fewer balls in play) confuse new fans more than they engage them. Recurring user feedback: "slow pace of play," "lengthy play time," "difficulty understanding the game."

The core issue: a disconnect between explanation and recognition. New fans lacked intuitive pitch-type identification skills, making the sport feel inaccessible.

02

Research & Insights

Methods

  • 8 unstructured interviews with casual viewers (ages 25–30).
  • Competitive analysis of MLB Jr., YouTube, and existing sports AR tools.
  • SWOT mapping interactive learning gaps.
  • Persona refinement for younger audiences.

Key findings

  • Participants described live pitching as "blurry" and hard to track.
  • Interactive visual exposure helped them identify pitch differences.
  • Guessing and testing made learning feel less formal and more enjoyable.
  • Existing tools felt either too passive or overly technical.
03

Design Approach

If new fans could intuitively identify pitch types, would that increase engagement?

Design priorities

  • Encourage curiosity over correctness.
  • Replace instruction with interaction.
  • Reinforce perception through motion and repetition.

Strategic considerations

  • Leverage 3D space (z-axis) for spatial ball trajectories.
  • Remove login barriers for accessibility.
  • Incorporate light gamification for engagement.
  • Promote adoption through context-based distribution — QR codes on tickets.
04

Key Design Decisions

  • Guess-first interaction flow — users watch a pitch and guess before getting feedback, encouraging pattern recognition over memorization.
  • Six commonly used pitch types — fastball, slider, cutter, sinker, changeup, curveball.
  • Strike-zone-accurate trajectories — realistic spatial paths in AR, not stylized arrows.
  • Subtle visual / audio feedback — soft cues without explicit scoring, so the experience feels exploratory.
  • Replayable pitch loops — multiple angles and speeds for repeated exposure.
05

Implementation

From userflow sketch to lo-fi Figma to hi-fi prototype to a working Unity / Niantic Lightship build.

06

Usability Testing

Three rounds of in-person testing with casual fans aged 25–30, five participants per round.

  • Round 1 — validate pitch motion clarity.
  • Round 2 — improve home screen and immersion.
  • Round 3 — assess pitch-distinguishing confidence.

Observations

  • Most participants noticed motion differences after repeated exposure.
  • The guess-first flow felt less intimidating than direct instruction.
  • Home interface improvements measurably reduced friction.
  • Multiple participants expressed a desire to learn more across sessions.
07

Live Demo

Mobile AR demo — guess the pitch, watch the spatial trajectory, and learn through repeated motion exposure.

Phone-held demo of the AR pitch recognition flow.

08

Outcomes & Takeaways

"Immediately executable, with potential as an onboarding tool for new fans." — MLB Advanced Media Team

Outcomes

  • Functional AR prototype deployable on standard mobile devices.
  • Six pitch modules with real-world motion data.
  • Complete interaction flow with guess logic and feedback cues.
  • Participants reported greater confidence distinguishing pitch types — multiple users said the experience made baseball "less intimidating" and sparked new interest.

Takeaways

  • Prioritizing interaction over instruction turns confusion into curiosity.
  • AR can serve as a meaningful engagement tool beyond novelty — when accessible, intuitive XR design is treated as a discipline rather than a demo.
  • Learning through doing keeps users engaged with complex content longer than passive explanation.
