Project 01 · Children's Creative Learning

Fostering Curiosity
Through Creative Learning

Creative Learning Platform · Smilegate FutureLab Foundation

AhhaOhho is a creative learning platform that encourages children to explore hands-on activities, express ideas through tinkering, and learn from each other through shared projects and peer feedback. As product design lead at Smilegate FutureLab Foundation, I shaped the end-to-end experience — from the creative spiral loop to the social engagement layer. Tested with 3,000+ children. V1.0 launches June 2025.

The Problem

Children have the devices. What they lack is a space to be curious.

In Korea, academic pressure starts early. Most parents and students focus heavily on standardized testing from elementary school onward — leaving little room for open-ended exploration or creative risk-taking. Over 90% of 6th graders own smartphones, but for most children these devices default to passive content consumption rather than hands-on expression.

AhhaOhho is a creative learning platform that encourages children to discover making activities, share their own projects, and grow through peer feedback.

Design Approach

Every design decision traced back to one question: can a child complete the full creative loop on his/her own?

The core UX is built around Mitch Resnick's Creative Spiral , a iterative cycle of curiosity, making, reflecting, sharing.

Primary Experience : Creative Loop
Discover → Make → Reflect → Share → Feedback

Curiosity and peer feedback are the strongest motivators to spark young users' creative loop.

Facilitation Strategy : Interactive Emotional Scaffolding
  • Personalized content curation
  • Tailored questions
  • Chunk Instruction
  • Individualized AI feedback
Gamification Strategy
  • Narrative & Role Assignment : Users join a mission-driven world with characters and streak-based onboarding rewards that boost early engagement
  • Achievement : Dashboard displays badges, levels, and quest progress to help users track their growth and feel accomplishment
  • Creative Empowerment : Missions allow free expression, remixing, and profile decoration. AI-guided prompts scaffold hesitant users
  • Peer Interaction : Social feedback (e.g., emoji reactions, peer feed) builds relatedness and encourages iteration
03

3 design decisions
that shaped the product

Decision 01

Give every child a built-in facilitator.

Teachers couldn't guide 20+ students through an unfamiliar app simultaneously. Children needed a reason to care before they'd engage.

Introduced Ahha & Ohho — two mascot characters that act as the child's personal companion. They assign your first mission, walk you through challenges, celebrate uploads, and suggest what to try next. Think Duolingo's owl, but for creative learning.

Turned onboarding from a cold content menu into a guided narrative. Reduced teacher dependency for first-time users and gave the gamification system a meaningful entry point.

Decision 02

Close the creative loop inside one app.

At every school, the creative cycle broke at "share." Teachers used Padlet for posting, YouTube for context, external tools for making. The app was a starting point, never the full journey.

Redesigned the platform so the entire creative spiral lives in one place: discover a challenge, make something, post to the feed, reflect on it, share with peers, and get feedback — without ever leaving.

The feed is where the loop restarts — a peer's project sparks the curiosity that begins the next creation cycle. No more tool-switching. One continuous creative experience.

Decision 03

Build the social layer as the product's core, not a feature.

Peer recognition drove repeat creative output more than any content or reward mechanic. One teacher chose a competitor specifically for its community sharing.

Restructured the platform so reactions, peer visibility, and shared projects form the engagement spine — not a secondary tab, but the structural core every other feature connects to.

The social feed became the primary re-engagement driver. Students returned to see reactions on their posts and discover peers' work — creating organic revisit loops.

04

User Research

Field studies across schools, community centers, and a pop-up at the Busan International Kids & Youth Film Festival : 500+ children, 15 facilitators, and 8 workshop programs.

Key Findings

Sharing mattered more than making.

Students were motivated less by the activities themselves and more by the social layer around them — seeing peers' work, leaving reactions, getting noticed. At one school, the teacher chose a competitor specifically for its community sharing. This told me the engagement spine had to be social, not content.

Every classroom bent the app to fit — none used it as designed.

Teachers repurposed challenges, swapped in external tools for sharing, and compressed the app's role to a brief intro. The creative loop we designed wasn't wrong — but the platform forced teachers to complete it outside the app. The full cycle needed to live in one place.

Device access didn't mean interface fluency.

App crashes on older tablets, text-heavy screens that lost kids' attention, and navigation that required teacher intervention at every step. Designing for "children with smartphones" was the wrong frame. We needed to design for low-spec devices, short attention spans, and zero onboarding support.

Solution

The full creative loop: discover, make, post, reflect, share, feedback.

01

Discover

Interest-driven exploration through curated and trending challenges — the starting point of every creative loop.

02

Make

Step-by-step challenge flow with visual instructions, so children can follow at their own pace without teacher intervention.

03

Reflect

Guided prompts that help children look back at their work, name what they learned, and notice what to try next.

04

Personal gallery and social feed bring each child's projects into the community where peers can encounter them.

05

Feedback

Reactions, comments, and AI-guided cues from peers that spark the next cycle of curiosity.

Reflection

User tests showed that AhhaOhho could spark curiosity and creative agency in children, especially when they were able to make, document, and share their own interpretations. However, operating the product as a standalone service also revealed a key limitation: initial curiosity does not automatically lead to sustained engagement.

We learned that children need a clearer sense of purpose through missions, feedback, and growth. First-time users need a stronger onboarding path to understand the AhhaOhho universe and start creating without feeling lost. Facilitators also need practical tools that support group learning, not just individual exploration.

These learnings are shaping v1.0 — with a stronger social feedback layer, personalized AI content curation, and facilitator tools designed to support repeat participation in both individual and classroom settings.
